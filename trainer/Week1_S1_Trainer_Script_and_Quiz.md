### SLIDE 3 — What is Data? *(0:08 – 0:18)*

> **[Use this as your engagement hook — don't rush this slide]**

"Let me ask you something before I put anything on the screen in your head.

**'What did you do this morning before coming here?'**

> **[Pause for effect]**

"Checking your phone: your app usage time, location, battery level — all logged. The auto you took: GPS coordinates, trip duration, payment method — stored. Even the WhatsApp message you sent: timestamp, read receipts, device ID — recorded.

We are data-generating machines, and we don't even realise it.

So formally, **data is any raw fact that can be recorded and stored**. On its own, data is just... noise. But when you process it, organise it, and give it context — it becomes *information*. And when information drives a decision — that's *insight*.

Think of it this way:
- Data: '32°C, 95% humidity'
- Information: 'It's very hot and humid today'
- Insight: 'I should launch a cold drink promotion campaign today'

**[Ask the room]**: Can anyone give me another example of the same progression — from raw data to an insight?"

### SLIDE 5 — Three Types of Data *(0:24 – 0:30)*

"Data comes in three flavours. Think of it on a spectrum from 'easy to work with' to 'wild and unpredictable'.

**Structured data** is our best friend. Rows and columns. Tables. Every field has a label, every value has a type. SQL databases, Excel sheets — all structured. Like a perfectly organised filing cabinet.

**Semi-structured data** is like that colleague who's mostly organised but has their own system. It has *some* structure — like JSON or XML — but it's flexible. The field names exist, but the schema isn't rigid.

**Unstructured data** is the beast. Videos, images, social media posts, customer call recordings. 80% of the world's data is unstructured. It's the hardest to work with — and also where some of the richest insights hide.

**[Quick poll — show of hands]:**
- 'Who works with structured data at work right now?' *(likely most hands)*
- 'Who works with semi-structured or unstructured data?' *(fewer hands)*

As data engineers and modellers, you'll need to handle ALL three."

### SLIDE 7 — Enterprise Data Lifecycle *(0:36 – 0:42)*

"Now that we know *what* data is and *where* it comes from — let's talk about what actually happens to it in an enterprise.

This is the **Data Lifecycle** — the journey data takes from the moment it's created to the moment it changes a business decision.

**Step 1 — Collect.** Data enters the system. A customer places an order, a sensor fires, a form is submitted. Raw events.

**Step 2 — Store.** That data goes somewhere — a database, a data lake, a cloud bucket. If it's not stored properly, it's lost.

**Step 3 — Process.** Raw data is messy. This is where we clean it, transform it, and make it usable. *This is where you come in* — this is data engineering.

**Step 4 — Analyse.** Now analysts and BI tools make sense of it. Dashboards, queries, reports.

**Step 5 — Decide.** And finally — a business makes a smarter decision. Launch this product. Hire this team. Enter this market.

Your job as a Data Modeller sits primarily in Steps 2 and 3 — but you have to understand all five steps to do your job well.

**[Ask the room]**: 'Can anyone think of a scenario where skipping or rushing one of these steps causes problems downstream?'

*(Common answers: Not storing data → can't analyse. Not cleaning data → wrong decisions. Not deciding → wasted effort.)*"

### SLIDE 8 — Real-World Case Studies *(0:42 – 0:45)*

"Let me show you this in the real world — because data isn't just a tech thing. It's a business thing.

**Retail (Amazon):** Every click, every scroll, every 'add to cart' is captured. Amazon's recommendation engine — 'customers who bought this also bought...' — accounts for 35% of their revenue. That's one data pipeline generating billions of dollars.

**Healthcare:** ICUs now use predictive models built on real-time vital signs to identify patients who might go into sepsis before it happens. This is data literally saving lives.

**Banking:** Your bank doesn't manually check if your credit card transaction is fraudulent. A model trained on millions of past transactions flags it in milliseconds.

**Logistics:** Delivery companies optimise 50,000 routes per day using live traffic data, weather data, and package weight. Without data, this is impossible at scale.

**[Key point to land]**: In every case — data is not just a byproduct of the business. It IS the business."

### SLIDE 9 — Why Data Quality Matters *(0:45 – 0:49)*

"Here's something that trips up a lot of people who are new to data work. They assume that if data exists, it's usable. That's wrong.

IBM estimated that bad data costs organisations an average of **$12.9 million per year**. And the reason it's so expensive is this: by the time you realise your decision was based on bad data, you've already acted on it.

A data analyst spends up to 80% of their time just *cleaning data* before they can even start analysing. That's not analysis — that's damage control.

There's a classic rule in data: **1:10**. It costs 1 rupee to prevent a data quality problem. It costs 10 rupees to fix it after it's recorded. And 100 rupees if it reaches a downstream report.

**[Pair activity — 2 minutes]**: Turn to a colleague and think of a decision — business or personal — that went wrong because the information you had was incomplete or incorrect. What happened? What would you have done differently with accurate data?"

> **[Take 2-3 responses, connect back to the core message]**

### SLIDE 10 — Data Professional Landscape *(0:49 – 0:52)*

"Last concept before we wrap up — *your place in this world*.

The demand for data professionals has exploded. But not all data jobs are the same. Look at the six roles on screen:

- **Data Engineer**: Builds the pipelines. Your role.
- **Data Analyst**: Asks the questions. The consumer of your work.
- **Data Scientist**: Builds models. Works on top of your infrastructure.
- **BI Developer**: Creates dashboards. Also your role.
- **Data Architect**: Designs the system. Where you're heading.
- **Data Steward**: Governs the data. Your awareness domain.

You are training to be a **Data Modeller** — which means you sit at the intersection of Engineer and Architect. You design how data is structured and stored so that analysts and scientists can do their jobs efficiently.

By the end of this 16-week program, you will have the skills to contribute meaningfully in all of these roles."

**Q13.** Explain the difference between 'data', 'information' and 'insight'. Give a real-world example of each.

- Data: A raw temperature reading — "38.5°C"
- Information: "The patient has a fever" (data in context)

*(Accept any reasonable real-world example)*

---

**Q14.** A retailer collects millions of sales transactions daily. Briefly describe what happens at each stage of the data lifecycle for this scenario.

**Model Answer:**
- Collect: POS terminals record each transaction
- Store: Data goes to a central database or data warehouse
- Process: Data is cleaned, duplicates removed, standardised formats applied
- Analyse: Dashboards show daily revenue, top-selling products, regional trends
- Decide: Management adjusts stock levels, runs promotions, plans staffing

---

**Q15.** Why is data quality so important? Give one consequence of working with poor-quality data.

**Model Answer:** Data quality determines the reliability of all analysis and decisions that flow from it. Poor quality data can lead to wrong business decisions (e.g., ordering too much stock, targeting the wrong customers), financial losses, damaged reputation, or compliance failures.

## 🎮 INTERACTIVE ACTIVITY IDEAS FOR THE TRAINER

### 1. Kahoot / Mentimeter Quick Poll (5 mins)
Use Q1-Q7 in a live Kahoot game at the end of session. Learners vote on phones. Instant leaderboard creates healthy competition and reinforces concepts.

### 2. "Data Detectives" (Pair Activity — 5 mins)
Give pairs a scenario card:
- *Scenario A*: A coffee shop chain in 20 cities
- *Scenario B*: A government hospital with 500 beds
- *Scenario C*: A ride-hailing app like Ola/Uber

Ask: "What data does this organisation collect? What decisions could it make from that data?"

### 3. "Classify It!" Card Sort Game (Group Activity — 5 mins)
Print 12 data examples on cards. Teams sort them into Structured / Semi-Structured / Unstructured in the fastest time. Examples:
- Customer order table (Structured)
- A Google review (Unstructured)
- Product XML feed from supplier (Semi-Structured)
- Employee attendance sheet (Structured)
- Customer service call recording (Unstructured)
- API JSON response (Semi-Structured)

### 4. Real-Time Whiteboard Brainstorm (3 mins)
Draw a stick figure going through their morning. Ask learners to shout out every data point generated. Fill the whiteboard. Works beautifully to show how data is invisible but omnipresent.

### 5. Post-it Exit Ticket
Before learners leave, ask each person to write on a sticky note:
- ONE thing they learned today
- ONE question they still have

Stick them on the door. Read questions at the start of Session 2.
